# Introduction

For my first course in the Bentley University [Masters in Human Factors in Information Design](https://www.bentley.edu/academics/graduate-programs/masters-human-factors), all online students spent a week in Boston for the Innovation Bootcamp course.

This course was a fast-track through the various components of UX Research, such as, but not limited to: user requirements gathering, field methods, prototyping and usability testing. It allowed students to get a taste of the types of classes and professors through the lens of a particular research area.

# Course

Problem for the week: Caretakers (of those with disabilities) Navigating Public Spaces
We covered at a high level the types of cognitive and physical disabilities.

## Day 1

### Ethnography
We started the course with an day-long ethnographic study of tourist destinations in the Greater Boston area. Focusing on what was present and not present in areas related to the needs of caretakers. Some scenario situations were presented to stimulate areas of need:
- taking an elderly parent out for a picnic in Concord, MA or near the Charles River
- going to the Museum of Science for someone with autism
- traveling around the city with someone in a motorized wheelchair

#### Field Study - Concord, MA

In the field study of a hot day in Concord, MA, it was easy to understand that this destination was not setup for those with disabilities or their caretakers. The town made some effort to offer wheelchairs and listening devices for guided tours, but the town consisted of extremely old infrastructure suited more towards able-bodies.

While the study provided our team with some knowledge, more time spent studying in the field would allow for better in-context data. The study in concord occurred on a hot day, so there weren't as many people walking around in town to observe. The team would like to conduct studies with a larger population.

Observations/Questions
  1. Visitor center was hard to navigate to
  2. Elderly needing a place to brace their weight/stabilize their body
  3. Terrible cell service so no way to plan on the fly or in an offline mode
  4. Lots of seating areas
  5. Not many areas for water
  6. Lots of place to sit in the main areas but nothing in between the two
  7. Where can you give the town feedback (Opportunity)
  10. Needed more cross walks
  11. Do the police and areas have training around non-nuerotypical people?
  12. Website doesn’t have obvious tourist information
  13. Holding a map while managing someone else might be difficult
  14. Would be useful: Trails marked on the road Tour times are on the website
  15. Cell service - if caretakers wanted to put their kid on the iPad for a distraction, they might not have the ability to

## Day 2

### Interviews
We conducted 1 hour of group interviews with 5 caretakers. The following day we conducted 4, 1:1 interviews with caretakers. The interviews allowed us to develop personas.

Group Interview Takeaways:
  - Caretakers do a significant amount of planning
  - Caretakers conduct research online or by calling services and asking lengthy Questions
  - One caretaker did not look for disability-friendly events, she planned what she wanted to do for the entire family and made the needed arrangements to make it possible for her child with disabilities.
  - The Disney brand is regarded well due to their customer service and willingness to cater. Disney earned significant trust.
  - Trip advisor and Yelp were heavily used
  - When planning, checklists are often used
  - Many caretakers rely on referrals from other caretakers

### Personas

  - The teams built personas and combined the various personas to create 2-3 personas reflective of the group.

## Day 4

For conducting the usability testing later in the day, the class crafted moderator guides for testing. The guides consisted of open-ended questions and was conducted in a moderately structured way.

The questions in the guide would ask the users to complete a task, but would not give the user's any insight on how the task would be completed.

The testing used the POP prototypes which were low-fidelity.

The program conducted the recruiting and the incentive, so the class only had to conduct the test.

### Usability Testing
- Caretaker A
  - Cares for his elderly mother
  - Success is when he can take his mom to art/culture events successfully
  - City experiences can be difficult, for example, he has to drop her off at a location and then park the car (much further) and hope she is ok by herself.
  - Would do anything to make her happy
  - Prototype Takeaways:
    - Not necessarily useful to him as he already knows where he plans to take his mother, he isn't necessarily looking for suggestions.
    - The community features was not important to him, he and his mother are older, not looking to meet new people.
    - He would find value in places that navigate his experience once he has already decided on what to do. Something that assists with parking and navigating the venue.
    - This caretaker doesn't plan to contribute to sites, does not see the incentive in contributing.

  Usability Takeaways:
  - While the prototypes were low-fidelity, there were aspects that were still higher-fidelity than desired, so participants questioned some aspects that had to do with the level of fidelity presented.
  - Our prototype was designed for the persona of a caretaker who cares for young people, the people in the usability testing cared for elderly and the way they planned and attended activities was different.
